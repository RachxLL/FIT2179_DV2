Smoking Prevalence in Malaysia

How Malaysia’s smoking patterns differ across places, groups, and health burden

Smoking remains a major public health issue in Malaysia. National prevalence has declined, but the burden is uneven across states and groups.

Some states recorded much higher smoking rates in 2023, while men continued to smoke at far higher rates than women.

Smoking is also concentrated among working-age adults, while education and income reveal further social differences in tobacco use.

Together, these patterns show national progress, but also highlight communities where targeted tobacco control remains important.

Comparing both years suggests that national decline does not happen evenly across states. Some places show lower prevalence by 2023, but others still carry a heavier smoking burden.

This means state-level tobacco control may still be needed, especially in high-prevalence areas.

Why Myanmar remains highest

Myanmar remains at the top because tobacco control has historically been weaker than in many neighbouring countries. A SEATCA regional study reported that Myanmar ranked last in ASEAN tobacco control, with out-of-date policies, cheap cigarette access, and widespread tobacco use.

The article also notes that Myanmar had not regularly updated its tobacco-control policy and strategy, did not spend public money on tobacco control, and still had cheap cigarette access. These factors help explain why tobacco use stayed high, including 43.8% among men in a 2014 survey.

Part 2
Prevalence changed as tobacco control expanded

The prevalence timeline shows how smoking changed across survey years, while the policy timeline shows how Malaysia’s tobacco-control framework expanded through regulation, smoke-free areas, repeated amendments, and the 2024 public health act.

Trend: 2005–2023
Focus: 2011 & 2023

The decline after 2016 may be associated with overlapping policy, market, and behavioural changes around 2015–2016. In 2015, excise duties increased sharply, and GST added further price pressure, making legal cigarettes less affordable for many smokers.

By 2016, analysts expected tobacco volume to decline because prices had moved beyond the pain threshold.

At the same time, Malaysia strengthened tobacco control, while vaping, cessation support, and illicit cigarettes changed how some smokers responded.

Overall, the later decline should be read as overlapping pressures, rather than one single factor.

Part 3
Smoking differs strongly across groups

Gender and age show that smoking remains concentrated in specific groups. In 2011, male smoking prevalence was 36.4% compared to a much lower female rate. By 2023, the male rate had fallen to 35.7% — a very slow decline — while female prevalence remained at 0.9%.

The gap persists, reflecting gendered social norms where male smoking remains more accepted across Southeast Asia.

For age, NHMS 2011 found current smoking highest among the 20–59 age groups, while NHMS 2023 identified the 35–44 group specifically as the peak at 25.0%. This shift suggests that smoking is increasingly concentrated in working-age adults rather than spread broadly.

Together, these patterns show that tobacco control should not only focus on the national average, but also target adult men and working-age groups where burden remains highest.

Education shapes smoking behaviour across both survey years. In 2011, NHMS found that current smoking was highest among those with secondary education and lowest among those with no formal or primary education — suggesting secondary-educated adults had higher exposure and social normalisation.

By 2023, NHMS found that primary education became the highest-prevalence group at 25.4%, while tertiary-educated adults recorded lower rates. This shift may reflect that higher education increasingly protects against tobacco use through greater health literacy and awareness.

Tobacco product use also varies by age and education. This 2023 heatmap compares cigarette and tobacco product types across age groups and education levels, adding another layer to the group-based smoking story.

Data year: 2023

The heatmap shows that tobacco product use is uneven across both age and education groups. Darker cells indicate groups where product use is more common, helping the audience see which combinations carry heavier tobacco exposure.

Instead of showing one national average, the heatmap reveals how smoking behaviour changes across social groups. Working-age adults tend to show stronger tobacco use patterns, while older groups are generally lower.

This matches Malaysian evidence that adults aged 25–44 spend more on cigarettes than younger adults, while adults aged 65 and above spend less as health awareness becomes more important.

Education also matters: lower education groups tend to show heavier cigarette demand than tertiary-educated adults. Together, the heatmap shows that tobacco control should not only target age groups, but also consider education-related differences in tobacco behaviour.

Part 4
Smoking and vaping show different income patterns

Income reveals another social divide in tobacco behaviour. Current smoking is more concentrated among lower-income groups, while vaping follows a different pattern. The state-level income scatter keeps the smoking and income relationship together.

Data year: 2023

Income reveals another social divide in smoking behaviour. The chart suggests that smoking is more concentrated among lower-income groups, showing that tobacco use is not only a personal choice but also linked to socioeconomic conditions.

Malaysian evidence supports this pattern: adult men with monthly household income below RM2000 were more likely to smoke than those earning RM3000 and above.

This relationship may be connected to financial stress, lower access to health information, and higher exposure to work or living environments where smoking is more common. The study also notes that smoking can be used as a coping mechanism for stress, especially among lower socioeconomic groups.

This means tobacco control should not only raise awareness, but also consider affordability, stress, education, and targeted support for groups where smoking remains more common.

Part 5
Smoking connects to disease and cost burden

The earlier charts show who smokes more, and where smoking remains higher. This section explains why those patterns matter: smoking is linked to major diseases, and those diseases create treatment costs for both patients and healthcare providers.

This pathway diagram shows how smoking moves from behaviour into wider health and economic consequences. The earlier charts identify who smokes more and where smoking remains common. This idiom explains why those patterns matter: smoking is associated with serious chronic diseases.

These include COPD, lung cancer, and ischaemic heart disease. Once these diseases occur, the burden continues through healthcare costs, including patient costs and provider costs. This makes smoking not only an individual health risk, but also a system-level cost issue.

The cost burden chart shows that smoking-related healthcare spending is highly concentrated in COPD. Among the three selected diseases, COPD accounts for about RM2.25 billion, making it the largest contributor to the total RM2.92 billion smoking-attributable treatment cost. This finding is important because smoking is often discussed mainly through lung cancer risk.

However, the cost evidence shows that chronic respiratory disease creates a much heavier treatment burden in this dataset. Ischaemic heart disease and lung cancer still contribute substantial costs, but COPD places the strongest financial pressure on the healthcare system. This reinforces the need for prevention and smoking cessation before long-term disease treatment becomes necessary.

Data and Sources

Smoking data: This page uses Malaysian smoking prevalence and e-cigarette or vape use data from national tobacco and health survey materials. The figures describe adults aged 15 years and above.

Map data: Malaysia state boundary data was adapted from a public GitHub TopoJSON source for educational use. State names were matched with smoking prevalence values to show the geographic pattern.

Healthcare cost data: The cost idioms use Malaysian smoking-related healthcare cost estimates for COPD, lung cancer, and ischaemic heart disease.

Reading the page: The results show differences in smoking prevalence across states, gender groups, age groups, education groups, income groups, tobacco product types, and time. They describe population-level patterns and should not be read as proving that one factor alone causes smoking behaviour.
