Smoking Prevalence in Malaysia

How Malaysia’s smoking patterns differ across places, groups, and health burden

Smoking remains a major public health issue in Malaysia. National prevalence has declined, but the burden is uneven across states and groups.

Some states recorded much higher smoking rates in 2023, while men continued to smoke at far higher rates than women.

Smoking is also concentrated among working-age adults, while education and income reveal further social differences in tobacco use.

Together, these patterns show national progress, but also highlight communities where targeted tobacco control remains important.

Part 1
Smoking is declining, but not evenly

The state map shows the 2011 and 2023 geographic patterns, while the ASEAN ranking places Malaysia within the regional tobacco-use context.

Data year: 2011 & 2023

Use the year slider to compare Malaysian states in 2011 and 2023. The map shows that smoking prevalence is uneven across place.

In 2023, higher rates appear in Sarawak, Perlis, Sabah, and Labuan. Lower rates appear in Selangor, Kuala Lumpur, Putrajaya, and Penang.

Comparing both years shows that decline is not evenly shared. Some places improved, while others still carry heavier smoking burden.

This supports targeted state-level tobacco control in high-prevalence areas.

Why Myanmar remains highest

Myanmar remains highest because tobacco control has historically been weaker than many neighbouring countries. SEATCA reported outdated policies, cheap cigarette access, and widespread tobacco use.

The same regional evidence noted limited policy updates and no public tobacco-control spending. These conditions help explain why tobacco use stayed high, including 43.8% among men in 2014.

Part 2
Prevalence changed as tobacco control expanded

The prevalence timeline shows long-term change, while the policy timeline shows how Malaysia’s tobacco-control framework expanded through regulation, smoke-free areas, amendments, and the 2024 public health act.

Trend: 2005–2023
Focus: 2011 & 2023

The decline after 2016 may reflect overlapping policy, market, and behavioural changes. Excise duties rose sharply in 2015, while GST added further price pressure.

By 2016, analysts expected tobacco volume to fall as prices passed the pain threshold.

At the same time, tobacco control strengthened, while vaping, cessation support, and illicit cigarettes changed smoker responses.

Overall, the later decline should be read as overlapping pressures, not one single cause.

Part 3
Smoking differs strongly across groups

Gender and age show that smoking remains concentrated in specific groups. Male smoking prevalence fell only slightly, from 36.4% in 2011 to 35.7% in 2023.

The gender gap persists, reflecting social norms where male smoking remains more accepted across Southeast Asia.

For age, NHMS 2023 identified the 35–44 group as the peak at 25.0%. This suggests smoking is concentrated among working-age adults.

Tobacco control should therefore target adult men and working-age groups, not only the national average.

Comparison:
2011 & 2023
Data year: 2023

Education shapes smoking behaviour across both survey years. In 2011, NHMS found that current smoking was highest among those with secondary education and lowest among those with no formal or primary education — suggesting secondary-educated adults had higher exposure and social normalisation.

By 2023, NHMS found that primary education became the highest-prevalence group at 25.4%, while tertiary-educated adults recorded lower rates. This shift may reflect that higher education increasingly protects against tobacco use through greater health literacy and awareness.

Data year: 2023

Tobacco product use also varies by age and education. This 2023 heatmap compares cigarette and tobacco product types across age groups and education levels, adding another layer to the group-based smoking story.

Data year: 2023

The heatmap shows uneven tobacco product use across age and education groups. Darker cells mark combinations with heavier tobacco exposure.

Instead of one national average, it shows how smoking behaviour changes across social groups. Working-age adults show stronger tobacco use patterns.

This matches Malaysian evidence that adults aged 25–44 spend more on cigarettes, while adults aged 65 and above spend less.

Education also matters. Lower education groups tend to show heavier cigarette demand than tertiary-educated adults.

Part 4
Smoking and vaping show different income patterns

Income reveals another social divide. Smoking is more concentrated among lower-income groups, while vaping follows a different pattern.

Data year: 2023

The chart suggests that smoking is more concentrated among lower-income groups. This frames tobacco use as linked to socioeconomic conditions, not only personal choice.

Malaysian evidence supports this pattern: adult men below RM2000 household income were more likely to smoke than those earning RM3000 and above.

This may connect to financial stress, lower health information access, and environments where smoking is more common.

Tobacco control should therefore consider affordability, stress, education, and targeted support.

Part 5
Smoking connects to disease and cost burden

The earlier charts show who smokes more and where prevalence remains higher. This section explains why those patterns matter: smoking is linked to disease and healthcare cost burden.

This pathway diagram shows how smoking moves from behaviour into wider health and economic consequences. It connects earlier prevalence patterns to serious chronic diseases.

These include COPD, lung cancer, and ischaemic heart disease. The burden then continues through patient and provider costs.

Treemap values show selected chronic disease shares of medically certified deaths. They do not show the percentage of deaths directly caused by smoking.

Data year: 2007

The cost burden chart uses 2007 estimates because a complete newer Malaysian dataset for these three smoking-attributable treatment costs could not be found.

Other newer sources were fragmented or incomplete, so 2007 is treated as the latest complete comparable source. COPD still shows the strongest cost burden.

Data and Sources

Smoking prevalence data: This dashboard uses Malaysian smoking prevalence and tobacco-use data from national survey reports, including NHMS 2011, GATS Malaysia 2011, NHMS 2023, GATS Malaysia 2023, and the GATS Malaysia 2011 and 2023 comparison fact sheet. These sources support the state map, gender comparison, age pattern, education pattern, and national smoking trend.

State and income data: State-level smoking prevalence was combined with household income data from OpenDOSM. The income-state scatter plot uses 2023 smoking prevalence and the latest available household income data by state, which is 2022.

Healthcare cost and disease burden data: The smoking-related healthcare cost idiom uses 2007 estimates from Health Care Costs of Smoking in Malaysia, which reports smoking-attributable treatment costs for COPD, lung cancer, and ischaemic heart disease. A complete newer Malaysian dataset for these three comparable treatment-cost estimates could not be found; other newer sources were fragmented or incomplete, so 2007 is used as the latest complete comparable source. Chronic disease death shares were supported by Department of Statistics Malaysia cause-of-death releases and time-series materials.

Policy and regional comparison data: The tobacco-control timeline uses Malaysia tobacco-control legislation information from Tobacco Control Laws. The ASEAN bump chart uses international smoking prevalence data from World Bank Open Data, supported by regional tobacco-control context from SEATCA.

Academic literature: The written interpretation is supported by Malaysian and regional studies on smoking prevalence, cigarette demand, gender differences, socioeconomic factors, adolescent tobacco use, and adult smoking behaviour. These studies help explain why smoking patterns differ by gender, age, income, education, and social context.
