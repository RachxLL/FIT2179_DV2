Smoking Prevalence in Malaysia

Where smoking remains common, and how the national picture has changed

Smoking remains a major public health issue in Malaysia. National prevalence has declined, but the pattern is uneven. Some states recorded much higher smoking rates in 2023. Men continued to smoke at far higher rates than women. Smoking was also highest among middle-aged adults, while education and income reveal further social differences. Together, these patterns show gradual progress, but uneven impact.

Part 2
Smoking differs strongly across groups

Gender is compared between 2011 and 2023, while age and education use 2023 data to show which groups were more affected in the latest survey year.

Gender gap

Men recorded far higher smoking prevalence than women in both 2011 and 2023. The gap remained large even after male smoking declined.

Age pattern

Smoking was highest among adults aged 35–44 in 2023. This separates the middle-age pattern from the gender pattern.

Education level

Smoking was highest among adults with primary education. This adds another social pattern to the smoking story.

The education-level pattern shows that smoking was not evenly distributed across educational groups.

Part 4
Smoking and vaping show different income patterns

Income gives another view of tobacco behaviour. This 2023 comparison shows that current smoking and e-cigarette or vape use do not follow the same income pattern.

Income pattern

Current smoking was higher among adults in the Bottom 40% household income category. In contrast, e-cigarette and vape use was higher among the Top 20% group.

Why this matters

This suggests that cigarette smoking and newer nicotine products may affect different income groups. They should not be explained as one identical behaviour.

Data and Sources

Smoking data: This page uses Malaysian smoking prevalence and e-cigarette or vape use data from national tobacco and health survey materials. The figures describe adults aged 15 years and above.

Map data: Malaysia state boundary data was adapted from a public GitHub TopoJSON source for educational use. State names were matched with smoking prevalence values to show the geographic pattern.

Reading the page: The results show differences in smoking prevalence across states, gender groups, age groups, education groups, income groups, tobacco product types, and time. They describe population-level patterns and should not be read as proving that one factor alone causes smoking behaviour.
